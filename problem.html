Chapter 02 · The Problem

When Your Data
Lies in Clusters

Standard regression has one critical assumption: every observation is independent. For repeated-measures data — 100 patients each recorded 26 times — this assumption is catastrophically wrong. Here's exactly what breaks, why it breaks, and what the alternatives miss.

1
The Assumption OLS Makes

What "independent" actually means — and why it fails

OLS assumes residuals are distributed as N(0, σ²I). That bold I — the identity matrix — encodes one claim: the covariance between any two residuals is exactly zero. Every row of your dataset is as unrelated to every other as two strangers pulled at random from the population.

Fine if each row is a different person, measured once. In RADAR-CNS, Patient 7's four rows look like this:

# Patient 7 — four rows, one person patient_id week phq8 pause_z 7 2 14 0.82 7 4 16 1.05 7 6 11 0.63 7 8 17 1.21 # OLS treats these as 4 independent people. They are not.

Patient 7 may simply have a naturally slow speaking style. Their pause lengths will be elevated across all weeks regardless of PHQ-8 — because that's who they are, not because of depression. This creates systematic within-row correlation that OLS cannot model, and treats as signal when it is only identity. Three precise consequences follow:

5×
Standard error deflation
With ICC = 0.96 and n=26 per patient, OLS underestimates the true SE by roughly √(1 + 25 × 0.96) ≈ 5-fold. A true SE of 0.50 appears as 0.10.
↑ t
Inflated t-values
Because SE is too small, t = β/SE is too large. A real t of 1.4 (non-significant) gets reported as t = 7.0. The p-value becomes essentially zero — a complete fiction.
≈ OK
But β is roughly right
The coefficient estimate β itself is not badly biased. OLS will estimate roughly the right effect size. The problem is the uncertainty around it — the confidence interval is fictionally narrow.

Live Simulation

See it happen: the false positive factory

3
Three Simpler Alternatives — and Why Each Fails

Why not just do something simpler?

Three alternatives to LMMs are commonly reached for. Each solves part of the problem but breaks something else in the process.

A
Aggregate
one row per patient
Partial fix

Average Patient 7's rows into one. Now 100 independent observations — independence satisfied.

What breaks: All within-patient information is gone. You can no longer ask "when Patient 7 feels worse, do their pauses get longer?" You can only ask "do patients who are worse on average also pause more on average?" — a fundamentally weaker, different question that can be confounded by stable personality differences rather than depression itself.

You also discard 96% of your data: 2,600 rows → 100.

B
Separate models
one regression per patient
Partial fix

Fit 100 separate regressions, one per patient, using only that patient's 26 rows.

What breaks: Each model has n=26 — extremely noisy estimates. You end up with 100 different β values with no principled way to combine them into a single, publishable answer about people with MDD in general.

The insight: LMM is a principled version of this idea — it fits per-patient intercepts while pooling the slope estimate towards a shared population value. It does what you were trying to do, correctly.

C
Ignore clustering
OLS on all 2,600 rows
Dangerous

Run one OLS on all rows. The β may be roughly correct — but the standard error will be too small, because OLS believes you have 2,600 independent observations rather than 100 independent patients.

What breaks: t-values are inflated. A t of 2.5 looks significant — accounting for clustering, the correct t might be 1.4. You publish a finding that does not exist.

In the Cummins et al. (2023) RADAR-MDD paper, several pause features found "significant" in earlier studies using inadequate methods became non-significant once proper LMMs were fitted. Those prior results were false positives.

4
Simpson's Paradox

When the overall trend points the wrong direction

This is the most striking demonstration of what goes wrong when clustering is ignored. In the chart below, the naïve OLS trend says pause length increases over sessions. The within-patient reality is the complete opposite: every single patient's pause length decreases over time as they improve. The apparent positive trend is a statistical illusion caused by sicker patients (higher baselines) coincidentally having more sessions — a confound that OLS cannot see, but LMM dissolves immediately by giving each patient their own intercept.
