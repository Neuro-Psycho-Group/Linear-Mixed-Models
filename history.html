Chapter 01 · History

140 Years to
One Equation

LMMs didn't appear from nowhere. They are the answer to a series of increasingly awkward questions that statisticians kept stumbling into. Understanding the history tells you exactly what problem each piece of the machinery solves.

Reading Strategy

Each entry in this timeline ends with a "what was still broken" note. That broken thing is exactly what the next entry fixes. By the end, you'll see why the LMM equation has every term it has — and nothing more.

1886
Galton discovers "regression to the mean" — and accidentally invents regression

Francis Galton was studying heredity — specifically, whether tall parents produce tall children. He noticed something counterintuitive: the children of very tall parents were tall, yes, but on average less tall than their parents. Children of very short parents were short, but less short. Everything regressed toward the average. He called this "regression toward mediocrity."

To describe this mathematically, Galton drew a line through his scatter plot. This was the first regression line. His student Karl Pearson later formalised it. The idea: you have some input X (parent height), some output Y (child height), and you fit a line: Y = a + bX.

The seed planted: data has systematic structure (the line) plus noise around it. The line is the signal; the scatter around it is what you can't explain.

What was still broken: The method assumed all observations were independent. Galton's children were independent (different people). But what if your data had groups — multiple measurements on the same person, or students within classrooms?

1919–1925
Fisher invents ANOVA — and the concept of variance partitioning

R.A. Fisher was working at Rothamsted agricultural station, analysing crop experiments. He had a key insight: the total variability in your data can be chopped up (partitioned) into pieces, each attributed to a different source. Rainfall variation, fertiliser variation, plot-to-plot variation. He called this Analysis of Variance — ANOVA.

ANOVA introduced the F-test, allowing you to ask: "Is the variance between groups (e.g., different fertiliser treatments) bigger than the variance within groups (random noise within each treatment plot)?" If yes, the treatment has a real effect.

The crucial new idea: variance itself is informative, not just means. And variance can be split into components — between-group and within-group. This concept lives at the heart of every LMM today.

What was still broken: ANOVA required balanced designs (equal group sizes) and couldn't handle continuous predictors alongside categorical ones. It also had no mechanism for "random" groups — in agricultural experiments, Fisher treated each field as a fixed, known treatment level. But what if you're sampling random doctors from a population of doctors, and you want to generalise beyond those specific doctors?

1950
Henderson introduces "random effects" in animal breeding

C.R. Henderson was a statistician for the dairy industry with a concrete problem: given milk yield data from many cows across many farms, how do you estimate the genetic value of a bull who has fathered cows across different farms?

The farm matters — a good farm produces more milk regardless of genetics. But farms weren't the thing of interest; they were just noise to be controlled. Henderson had the key conceptual breakthrough: treat farms as having a random effect, not a fixed one.

Fixed effect means: these are the specific levels we care about, and we estimate a coefficient for each. If you have 5 fertiliser treatments, you get 5 coefficients and you care about each one individually.

Random effect means: these are a random sample from a larger population of groups. We don't care about any specific farm's value — we just want to account for farm-to-farm variability so our estimate of the bull's genetic value is clean. We model the farm effects as being drawn from a normal distribution with some variance.

This is the "mixed" in mixed model — mixing fixed effects (things you specifically care about) with random effects (group structure you need to control for). A model with only fixed effects is just regression. A model with only random effects is just a variance components model. LMM has both.

What was still broken: Henderson's estimation method had bias issues. The variance components (how much variability is due to farms? how much due to genetics?) were estimated with systematic error. This would take another 25 years to fix.

1982
Laird & Ware unify everything into the modern LMM framework

Nan Laird and James Ware published "Random-Effects Models for Longitudinal Data" — the paper that defined the modern LMM. They brought together regression, variance partitioning, and random effects into one coherent framework, and crucially, gave it an estimation method that actually worked for longitudinal data (repeated measurements over time).

Their formulation: Y = Xβ + Zu + ε — with precise statistical definitions for what each piece is and how to estimate it. This is the equation you're learning. Every software package implementing LMMs (R's lme4, Python's statsmodels, SAS PROC MIXED) is implementing the Laird-Ware framework.

Why 1982 specifically? By then, computers were powerful enough to fit these models numerically. Before that, the mathematics existed but was too computationally expensive to use in practice.

What was still broken: The estimation of variance components (σ² for random effects, σ² for residuals) was biased using standard maximum likelihood. ML underestimates variance because it doesn't account for the degrees of freedom consumed by estimating the fixed effects β first. Enter REML.

1971 → widely adopted 1980s
REML — the fix for biased variance estimation

Patterson and Thompson developed Restricted Maximum Likelihood (REML) for the specific problem of estimating variance components without bias. Understanding why it works requires understanding what ML gets wrong first.

// Degrees of Freedom — What They Actually Are

The phrase "degrees of freedom" gets used constantly in statistics and almost never explained intuitively. Here is the real idea.

A degree of freedom is one independent piece of information in your data that is free to vary. When you use some of your data to estimate a parameter, you "spend" degrees of freedom — and the data becomes constrained. Constrained data contains less independent information than you think.

// The Concrete Example: n=4 numbers, mean=5

Say you have 4 numbers whose mean is 5. You are told the first three: 3, 7, 4. What must the fourth number be?

3 + 7 + 4 + x = 4 × 5 = 20
x = 20 − 14 = 6  ← not free. forced.

The fourth value had no freedom — once you knew the mean and the other three values, it was determined. You had 4 numbers, but only 3 independent pieces of information. The constraint "mean = 5" ate one degree of freedom.

Figure: Four data points on a number line. The mean (▼) is fixed at 5. The first three points (blue) are free to be anywhere. Once they are placed, the fourth (red) is locked — it has no freedom. The mean constraint is like a see-saw: both sides must balance.

// Why This Makes the Variance Formula Use n−1

Sample variance = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / ?

You might think: divide by n, because there are n squared deviations. But you calculated x̄ from the same data. By doing so, you forced a constraint: the deviations (xᵢ − x̄) must sum to exactly zero. Always. No exceptions.

(3−5) + (7−5) + (4−5) + (6−5) = −2 + 2 + (−1) + 1 = 0 ✓
This is not a coincidence. It is always true when x̄ is the sample mean.

Because the deviations must sum to zero, the last deviation is determined by all the others. You have n deviations but only n−1 are independent. Dividing by n treats all n as independent — it underestimates the true variance. Dividing by n−1 corrects for the one degree of freedom spent on estimating x̄. This is called Bessel's correction.

// Generalising: p Parameters = p Degrees of Freedom Spent

In OLS regression with p predictors (including intercept), you estimate p coefficients from n data points. Each coefficient you estimate uses up one degree of freedom — it imposes one linear constraint on the residuals.

OLS residual variance = Σεᵢ² / (n − p)
n observations, minus p parameters estimated, leaves n−p free residuals

For an LMM with p fixed-effect parameters, Maximum Likelihood ignores this and divides by n — as if all n residuals are free. This is wrong: the p fixed-effect estimates constrain the residuals, just like x̄ constrained the deviations. REML accounts for this by working in a transformed space where the fixed effects have been projected out — leaving n−p truly free observations for variance estimation.

The Punchline for REML

ML estimates β and σ² simultaneously — it does not account for the degrees of freedom spent on β when estimating σ². REML first projects out β (mathematically removes its influence), then estimates σ² from the remaining n−p degrees of freedom. This gives unbiased variance component estimates — crucial for correct ICC values, correct standard errors on fixed effects, and valid inference.

ML (biased)
Estimate everything at once
Maximises the full data likelihood jointly over β and σ². Treats all n observations as free for variance estimation. With p=5 fixed parameters and n=100, acts as if df=100. Actually df=95. Underestimates σ² by factor of 95/100 — small but meaningful, especially with many fixed effects.
REML (unbiased) ✓
Project out fixed effects first
Maximises a restricted likelihood in the n−p dimensional space orthogonal to β. Concretely: transforms data into n−p error contrasts containing no β information. Estimates σ² from these contrasts using the correct df=n−p. Result: unbiased variance, correct standard errors, valid p-values.

The Practical Rule You'll Actually Use

When you fit a model in R: lmer(y ~ x + (1|patient), data=d, REML=TRUE) is the default for reporting. If you need to compare whether adding PHQ-8 as a predictor improves the model, refit with REML=FALSE first, then use anova(model1, model2). The lmerTest package handles p-values for you using Satterthwaite-corrected degrees of freedom.

2005 → present
Douglas Bates writes lme4 — LMMs become accessible

Before lme4, fitting LMMs required either SAS (expensive, syntactically painful) or writing your own Newton-Raphson optimiser. Bates and colleagues built an R package that could fit LMMs in seconds using sparse matrix algebra and modern REML algorithms. Suddenly, longitudinal data analysis with random effects was something any researcher could do.

The formula syntax y ~ x + (1|group) — read as "y predicted by x, with a random intercept per group" — became the standard way to specify mixed models. Variants like (1 + time|patient) add random slopes.

The journey: Galton's scatter plot (1886) → Fisher's variance partitioning (1925) → Henderson's random effects (1950) → Laird-Ware framework (1982) → REML (widely adopted 1980s) → lme4 (2005) → your RADAR-CNS analysis (now).
