Data Types · When LMMs Apply

Four Kinds of
Awkward Data

LMMs exist because real-world data is messy in specific, predictable ways. Non-independent, hierarchical, longitudinal, clustered — these four words describe the same underlying problem from different angles. This page shows you exactly what each looks like, with diagrams.

🔗

One Root Cause, Four Names

All four labels describe data where the independence assumption of OLS is violated — observations share some common source of variation (a person, a school, a time period) that makes them more similar to each other than to random observations from outside their group. LMMs handle this by explicitly modelling the group-level variation as random effects.

Type 01

Longitudinal Data

The same individuals are measured multiple times, typically at regular intervals. Each person contributes a trajectory over time, not just a single data point.

// Diagram: 5 patients, 4 timepoints each — individual trajectories
Each coloured line = one patient's trajectory. They start at different places (different baselines) and change at different rates. OLS would collapse this to one line — losing both baseline differences and trajectory information.

What makes it longitudinal

  • Same subjects, multiple timepoints. Patient 7 at week 2, week 4, week 6 — three rows, one person.
  • Rows within a person are correlated. Week 2 and week 4 for the same patient share their personality, biology, and speaking style. They are not independent.
  • The trajectory is often the thing of interest. Not just "what is their pause length?" but "how does their pause length change as their depression changes?"
  • Missing timepoints are the norm, not the exception. Patients drop out, miss appointments. LMMs handle unbalanced longitudinal data; repeated-measures ANOVA does not.
Real examples
Depression PHQ-8 over 52 weeks Blood pressure every 6 months Child reading scores each year Acoustic features per clinical visit Brain volume across aging
LMM Solution

Random intercept per patient absorbs each person's baseline. Fixed effect of time captures average trajectory. Random slope per patient allows trajectories to differ in steepness. Residual covariance structure (AR(1)) can model that closer timepoints are more correlated than distant ones.

Type 02

Clustered Data

Observations are grouped into clusters — they share a common context that makes them more similar to each other than to observations in other clusters. The cluster is not necessarily ordered in time.

// Diagram: 4 clusters (circles), observations within each
Points within each circle are more similar to each other than to points in other circles. The between-cluster spread is large; within-cluster spread is smaller. ICC measures this ratio.

What makes it clustered

  • Observations share a context. Patients in the same hospital share the same doctors, equipment, and protocols. Students in the same classroom share the same teacher. This shared context makes their outcomes correlated.
  • Clustering is not about time. You might collect all cluster data at the same moment — a one-off survey of pupils across many schools. It's still clustered because pupils share a school.
  • Ignoring clusters inflates your sample size artificially. A survey of 500 students in 10 classrooms is not the same as 500 independent people. The effective N is somewhere between 10 and 500.
  • The ICC quantifies how severe the clustering is. ICC = 0 means no clustering at all. ICC = 1 means everyone in a cluster is identical.
Real examples
Patients within hospitals Students within schools Employees within companies Soil samples within fields Animals within litters
LMM Solution

Random intercept per cluster absorbs the shared cluster-level context. The fixed effects then estimate what predicts the outcome after controlling for cluster membership. The SE on fixed effects is correct — not inflated by treating clustered observations as independent.

Type 03

Hierarchical (Nested) Data

Data has multiple levels of structure, each nested inside the next. Students inside classrooms inside schools inside districts. Observations inside patients inside hospitals inside countries.

// Diagram: 3-level hierarchy — observations → patients → hospitals
Each level introduces a new source of variability. Hospital differences, patient differences within hospitals, and observation noise within patients are all separate variance components.

What makes it hierarchical

  • Multiple levels, each nested inside the next. Level 1: individual observations. Level 2: patients. Level 3: hospitals. Each level is a random sample from a larger population.
  • Variability exists at every level. Some hospitals are better than others (hospital-level variance). Some patients respond better than others within the same hospital (patient-level variance). Some visits are noisier than others (residual variance).
  • Cross-level interactions are possible. Does the hospital type moderate the patient-level treatment effect? These cross-level questions are unique to hierarchical models.
  • Higher levels provide context for lower levels. A patient's outcome might depend both on their own characteristics AND on which hospital they're in. The model needs to know about both.
Real examples
Pupils → classrooms → schools Patients → doctors → hospitals Repeated measures → patients → clinics Animals → litters → farms
LMM Solution

Add a random intercept at each level: (1|hospital/patient) in R syntax. This estimates the variance at each level separately and propagates correct uncertainty through all levels. You get hospital-level and patient-level variance components, and fixed effects that are adjusted for both.

Type 04 · The Root Concept

Non-Independent Data

The general case that contains all the others. Two observations are non-independent if knowing one tells you something about the other — if their residuals are correlated after accounting for the predictors.

// Correlation heatmap: residuals from a 12-row dataset with 3 patients
Low correlation
High correlation
Dark blocks along the diagonal = within-patient rows are highly correlated with each other. Off-diagonal light cells = across-patient rows are uncorrelated. OLS assumes the entire matrix is identity (no correlation anywhere). It's clearly not.

The OLS independence assumption — spelt out

OLS assumes the residuals ε follow N(0, σ²I). The I (identity matrix) says: the covariance between any two residuals εᵢ and εⱼ is zero. Every observation is as unrelated to every other as two strangers.

When data is longitudinal, clustered, or hierarchical, this is false. The actual residual covariance matrix looks like the heatmap on the left — a block-diagonal structure where within-group residuals are correlated.

LMMs model this explicitly. By adding random effects u, the implied covariance of Y becomes:

Var(Y) = Z·G·Zᵀ + R
Z·G·Zᵀ = between-group variance (block structure)
R = within-group residual variance

This matches the heatmap structure exactly. The LMM is, at its core, a method for estimating the correct covariance structure of Y — and then doing regression in a way that respects that structure.
