Chapter 03 · The Math

Building the
Equation
from Scratch

We'll start with a tiny real dataset — three patients, two observations each — and build every matrix in Y = Xβ + Zu + ε by hand. By the end, Z will make complete sense.

1
The Dataset

Start concrete: three patients, six rows

Abstract matrix algebra is hard to grasp in the abstract. So let's use the smallest possible real example: three patients (Alice, Bob, Cara), each measured twice, with one predictor (PHQ-8 score) and one outcome (delta-normalised pause length — written pause_z in the RADAR-CNS code).

Quick note: what pause_z is — and what the z is not

pause_z is a Z-score of pause duration, computed relative to each patient's own baseline. If Alice's average pause at her first few sessions was 0.4 s (±0.1 s SD), then a session where she pauses 0.6 s gives pause_z = (0.6 − 0.4) / 0.1 = +2.0. She is pausing 2 of her own standard deviations above her personal norm.

The z in pause_z is Z-score — a standardisation term meaning "how many SDs from a reference mean." It has nothing to do with the Z matrix in Y = Xβ + Zu + ε. That Z is the random-effects design matrix (the membership routing table for patients). Two completely different Z's from two different traditions that happen to coexist in the same analysis. In the Cummins et al. (2023, KCL / RADAR-MDD) paper, features including pause duration were standardised to mean 0, SD 1 before LMM fitting — so the β coefficients are interpretable as "per 1 SD change in the feature."

Building Xβ
Xβ Fixed effects — the relationship that applies to everyone

X is your design matrix of predictors — exactly like in OLS. Column 1 is always a column of 1s (for the intercept). Column 2 is PHQ-8. β is the vector of coefficients we want to estimate.

The key point: β does not change per patient. The same β₁ (effect of PHQ-8) applies to Alice, Bob, and Cara. It's the population-level relationship. That's why it's "fixed."

Interpretation

If estimation gives us β₀ = −0.5 and β₁ = 0.08, then for any patient with PHQ-8 = 14, the population-average predicted pause is −0.5 + (0.08 × 14) = 0.62 SD above baseline. This is the signal that applies to everyone. But Alice's actual value (0.82) is higher than that prediction — because Alice personally tends to pause more. That personal offset is what the random effect captures.

4
The Key: Z and u
Zu Random effects — per-patient offsets from the population line

The question Zu answers

After Xβ gives us the population-average prediction, there's still leftover structure in our data. Alice's rows are consistently above the population line. Bob's are consistently below. This isn't random noise — it's systematic and it belongs to each person.

We need to add a person-specific offset to each row. Call these offsets u_Alice, u_Bob, u_Cara. If Alice tends to pause more than average, u_Alice will be positive. If Bob tends to pause less, u_Bob will be negative.

What is u?

u is a vector of those person-specific offsets. One per patient — three patients, so u is a 3×1 vector:

u (3×1) — random intercepts
u_Alice
u_Bob
u_Cara
← "Alice pauses +0.35 SD more than average" ← "Bob pauses −0.18 SD less than average" ← "Cara pauses +0.52 SD more than average"

These are assumed drawn from a normal distribution: u ~ N(0, σ²_u). That's the "random" part — we treat Alice, Bob, and Cara as a random sample from a population of patients, not specific fixed levels. We don't estimate u_Alice directly; we estimate σ²_u (how much patients vary in general) and then infer each person's offset.

What is Z?

Here's the key question: when we want to add u_Alice's offset to Alice's rows in Y, how does the model know which rows belong to Alice?

That's exactly what Z does. Z is a membership table — it tells the model which rows in Y belong to which patient in u. Click the patient buttons to see how Z maps observations to patients:

The "aha" moment for Z

When you multiply Z × u, each row of the result gets exactly the u value for the patient that row belongs to. Row 1 (Alice's first observation) gets u_Alice. Row 3 (Bob's first) gets u_Bob. The Z matrix is literally asking: "For this row in Y, which patient should I paste their personal offset from?" The 1s are the "yes, this row belongs to this patient." The 0s are "not this patient."

In real data with 100 patients and 2,600 rows, Z is a 2,600 × 100 matrix — very sparse (mostly zeros), which is why software uses efficient sparse matrix operations.

Why not just add u to X?

You might wonder: couldn't we just add patient_id as a predictor in X? Yes — that would be a fixed effect for patient. But that means estimating a separate β for each of 100 patients. That's 100 parameters just for patient identity, consuming enormous degrees of freedom, and crucially — it assumes those 100 patients are the only patients that matter. LMMs instead say: "Patients are a random sample from a population. I'll estimate one variance parameter (σ²_u) that describes how spread out patient offsets are, and infer each patient's offset from the data." Much more parsimonious, and allows generalisation.

5
The Full Equation

One observation, fully expanded

Let's trace exactly what the model predicts for Alice's first observation (Row 1: PHQ-8 = 14, Y = 0.82):

// Prediction for Alice, Week 2
Y₁ = [β₀ + β₁ × 14] + [u_Alice] + [ε₁]
0.82 = [−0.50 + 0.08 × 14] + [+0.26] + [−0.06]
0.82 = 0.62  + 0.26 + (−0.06)

0.62 = "A person with PHQ-8=14 is expected to be 0.62 SD above their own baseline"
+0.26 = "Alice personally tends to pause 0.26 SD more than the average patient"
−0.06 = "On this specific day, Alice was slightly less affected than expected"
6
Variance Components & ICC

How much variation is between vs. within patients?

The LMM estimates two variance components: σ²_u (how much patients differ from each other) and σ²_ε (how much observations vary within a patient after accounting for fixed effects). Their ratio tells you something crucial.

// Variance split — adjust with slider

ICC (Intraclass Correlation Coefficient) = σ²_u / (σ²_u + σ²_ε)



ICC = 0.60
60% of variance is due to stable patient identity. This is high — random effects are essential.
// What ICC means for your analysis

ICC near 0: Patients don't differ much from each other. Most variance is within-patient. Random effects add little. A simpler model might suffice.

ICC 0.3–0.6: Substantial between-patient differences. Random effects are clearly needed. Your SE corrections will be meaningful.

ICC near 1: Almost all variance is between patients. Within-patient change is small relative to individual differences. This would mean your biomarker (within-patient change) has very little signal to detect.

In typical RADAR-CNS work, ICC for acoustic features tends to be moderate (0.3–0.6), meaning the model is capturing real individual differences while still having detectable within-patient signal.

7
Extension: Random Slopes

What if the PHQ-8 effect also varies per patient?

So far we've assumed: one PHQ-8 slope β₁ for everyone (fixed), but each person has a different baseline (random intercept). That's (1|patient) in R syntax.

But what if some patients show a strong vocal response to depression while others show almost none? You can add a random slope: let the PHQ-8 coefficient also vary by patient.

# Random intercept only (intercept varies per patient)
lmer(pause_z ~ phq8 + (1|patient))

# Random intercept + random slope (both vary per patient)
lmer(pause_z ~ phq8 + (1 + phq8|patient))

# What this means:
# u is now a 2-vector per patient: [intercept_offset, slope_offset]
# u_Alice = [+0.26, +0.03] — Alice: higher baseline, slightly steeper PHQ-8 response
# u_Bob = [-0.18, -0.01] — Bob: lower baseline, slightly flatter response

With random slopes, u becomes a 2×1 vector per patient (intercept offset + slope offset), and G becomes a 2×2 covariance matrix. The off-diagonal of G tells you: are patients with higher baselines also more reactive to depression? Interesting science lives in that covariance.
