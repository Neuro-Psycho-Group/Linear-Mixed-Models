Core Concept First

Random Intercepts vs. Random Slopes — what's the difference?

Before the workflows, you need to intuitively understand the two main types of random effects, because choosing between them is the central modelling decision.

// Model A
Random Intercepts Only
(1 | patient)
What it assumes: Every patient responds to the predictor (e.g. PHQ-8) by the same amount — the slope β₁ is identical for everyone. But each patient has their own baseline level (their intercept differs).

The lines are parallel. They start at different heights but climb at the same rate. The gap between patients stays constant across all PHQ-8 values.

Use when: You have reason to believe the effect is consistent across individuals. Most starting models begin here — it's simpler and requires fewer parameters.
// Model B
Random Intercepts + Random Slopes
(1 + phq8 | patient)
What it assumes: Each patient has their own baseline (intercept) AND their own rate of change with the predictor (slope). Some patients show a steep vocal response to depression; others barely change.

The lines fan out or cross. The gap between patients changes depending on the PHQ-8 value — they're not parallel.

Use when: Theory or exploratory plots suggest individual responses vary. It's richer but needs more data per person (rough guide: ≥5–10 observations per subject to estimate a random slope reliably).
The Practical Decision Rule

Start with random intercepts only. Check: do the residuals still show a pattern per subject (some always positive, some always negative)? If yes, random slopes may help. Also check: does adding random slopes significantly improve model fit (compare with likelihood ratio test using ML)? If you have <5 observations per subject, random slopes will likely fail to converge.

Longitudinal Data
01
Scenario: Longitudinal Study

Tracking depression over time

100 patients with MDD. PHQ-8 measured every 2 weeks for a year. Goal: does acoustic feature X change as depression severity changes?

  • 1
    Explore the data visually

    Plot each patient's trajectory (outcome vs time, coloured by patient). Look for: (a) do patients differ in their starting levels? → suggests random intercepts. (b) do the slopes differ? → suggests random slopes. (c) is there a general trend across everyone? → suggests a fixed time effect.

  • 2
    Compute the ICC before modelling

    Fit a null model (random intercept, no predictors) to get baseline ICC. If ICC < 0.05, the data is barely clustered — a simple approach may suffice. If ICC > 0.1, random effects are clearly needed.

    Null model: outcome ~ 1 + (1|patient) → ICC = σ²_patient / (σ²_patient + σ²_residual)
  • 3
    Add the predictor of interest as a fixed effect

    Add PHQ-8 score as a fixed effect. This tests: "on average, across all patients, does worse depression predict higher pause_z?" Control for session number (time) as a covariate to separate the depression effect from any general trend over time.

    Model: outcome ~ phq8 + session + (1|patient)
  • 4
    Consider whether individual responses vary (random slopes)

    Ask: is it scientifically plausible that some patients respond more strongly to depression than others in their voice? If yes, add a random slope for PHQ-8. Compare models using likelihood ratio test (refit with ML, not REML).

    Extended: outcome ~ phq8 + session + (1 + phq8|patient)
    LRT: does adding the random slope significantly improve fit?
  • 5
    Check model assumptions

    Plot residuals vs fitted values (no fan shape). Plot BLUPs (random intercepts) on a number line — should be approximately normal around zero. Check for outlying patients with unusually large BLUPs.

  • 6
    Report the fixed effect with confidence intervals

    Report β_phq8 (the biomarker effect), its SE, t-value, and 95% CI. If the CI excludes zero, you have a statistically validated biomarker. Report ICC to show why random effects were necessary.

    Report: β₁ = X.XX (SE = X.XX), t = X.XX, 95% CI [X.XX, X.XX]; ICC = 0.XX
2
Hierarchical / Nested
02
Scenario: Students within Schools

Does a teaching intervention work?

2,000 students across 40 schools. Half the schools get a new curriculum. Goal: does the intervention improve test scores?

  • 1
    Identify the levels and the unit of randomisation

    Levels: students (level 1) nested in schools (level 2). The intervention was randomised at the school level — entire schools got the curriculum or didn't. This is important: individual student assignment isn't independent, so treating them as independent would be wrong.

  • 2
    Fit the null model and compute the school-level ICC

    ICC here tells you what fraction of test score variability is due to which school a student attends. High ICC means school-level factors matter a lot — you'll need school-level random effects. Low ICC means schools are interchangeable.

    Null: score ~ 1 + (1|school) → ICC = σ²_school / (σ²_school + σ²_student)
  • 3
    Add the treatment as a level-2 (school-level) fixed effect

    The intervention varies between schools, not between students within schools. It is a level-2 predictor. Adding it tests: do schools that received the curriculum have higher average scores?

    Model: score ~ intervention + student_age + (1|school)
  • 4
    Consider cross-level interactions

    Does the intervention work better for older students than younger ones? This is a cross-level interaction: a student-level variable (age) moderating a school-level effect (intervention). Theoretically interesting, but requires large samples to detect.

    Interaction: score ~ intervention * student_age + (1|school)
  • 5
    Report variance at each level

    Report σ²_school and σ²_student separately, as well as ICC. This shows how much of the unexplained variance is between schools (contextual) vs within schools (individual). The fixed effect of intervention (with its correct SE) is your main finding.

3
Clustered Cross-Sectional
03
Scenario: Patients within Hospitals

Does a drug reduce readmission rates?

5,000 patients across 30 hospitals. Each patient seen once. Goal: does Drug A reduce 30-day readmission?

  • 1
    Recognise the clustering: patients within hospitals

    Patients in the same hospital share doctors, protocols, equipment, and patient mix. Their readmission rates are correlated within hospitals — not because of the drug, but because of hospital quality. Ignoring this would inflate your effective sample size.

  • 2
    Check whether outcome is normal or binary

    Readmission is binary (yes/no). A standard LMM assumes a normal outcome. For binary outcomes, you need a Generalised Linear Mixed Model (GLMM) with a logistic link function. See the GLMM page for details.

    Binary outcome → GLMM with logit link: glmer(readmit ~ drug + age + (1|hospital), family=binomial)
  • 3
    Add patient-level and hospital-level controls

    Patient-level: age, comorbidities, severity score. Hospital-level: size, urban/rural, teaching status. Adding both controls for the confounders at each level, isolating the treatment effect.

  • 4
    Report odds ratios with cluster-robust CIs

    For logistic GLMM, interpret the exponentiated coefficient as an odds ratio: "Drug A is associated with X times the odds of readmission compared to no drug, controlling for patient characteristics and hospital." Report hospital-level ICC to justify the random effects.

Why LMMs Beat the Alternatives

Advantages of LMMs

🎯
Handles Dependency
Explicitly models the correlation within groups via random effects. Standard errors on fixed effects are correct — not inflated by treating correlated observations as independent. The t-values and p-values you get are ones you can actually trust.
🕳️
Missing Data
REML estimation uses all available data for each subject. A patient who only contributes 4 of 26 sessions is not dropped — they still contribute to estimating the fixed effects and their own BLUP. Repeated-measures ANOVA requires complete data; LMM does not.
📉
Reduced Bias (False Positives)
By correctly partitioning variance into between-group and within-group components, LMMs prevent the inflated t-statistics that come from ignoring clustering. The improvement can be dramatic — a "significant" OLS result can become non-significant once clustering is accounted for.
🔍
Separates Within vs Between Effects
Fixed effects capture population-average relationships. BLUPs capture individual deviations. This separation allows you to ask both "does PHQ-8 predict pause length in general?" and "which patients deviate most from the average pattern?" — simultaneously.
📊
Unbalanced Designs
Groups don't need equal sizes. Some patients can have 26 sessions; others 8. Some schools can have 20 students; others 50. LMMs weight each group's contribution according to how much data it provides — more data = more influence on estimates.
🧩
Interpretable Components
Unlike a black-box machine learning model, LMM output is human-readable: β₁ = 0.107 means a 1-unit increase in PHQ-8 is associated with 0.107 SD increase in pause length. The ICC = 0.96 means 96% of variance is between-patient. Every number has a precise meaning.
When LMMs Are Not Enough

LMMs assume the outcome Y is approximately normally distributed (conditional on the random effects). For binary outcomes (yes/no), count data (number of events), or ordinal data (scale ratings), you need Generalised Linear Mixed Models — the topic of the next page.
