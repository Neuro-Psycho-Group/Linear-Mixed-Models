// Why This Paper
The single most relevant paper to your experiment

This is the paper you will almost certainly cite when writing up your own KCL voice study. It is the largest real-world, longitudinal speech study in clinical depression ever conducted at the time of publication — 585 participants with a confirmed history of MDD across three languages, recording themselves via smartphone every fortnight for 18 months, with fortnightly PHQ-8 assessments to track symptom severity.

The corresponding author is Dr Nicholas Cummins, your supervisor at KCL. Understanding this paper deeply — its choices, its findings, and its acknowledged limitations — is foundational context for whatever you will do next in his lab.

Crucially, the central statistical tool is exactly the LMM you have been studying: a random intercept model with PHQ-8 as the fixed predictor, one model per speech feature, estimated with the lme4 package in R. This makes the paper a perfect illustration of LMMs applied at scale to precisely the kind of data you will encounter.

Why it matters that LMMs were used — not simple correlation

Earlier studies (including many that found stronger pause effects) used simple correlations or group comparisons. They did not account for the clustering of repeated measures within the same person. This paper explicitly corrects for this using random intercepts — and as a result, some previously "significant" findings (notably pause-related features) became non-significant. The LMM gave more honest, if sometimes less dramatic, answers.

// Study timeline — one participant
Each participant completed fortnightly cycles: a smartphone speech recording followed immediately by a PHQ-8 questionnaire. Over 18 months, this generates ~39 paired (speech, PHQ-8) datapoints per participant — a dense longitudinal dataset within each person.
// Study Design
What they did and how
// Three cohorts, two speech tasks
Participants were split across three language cohorts. Each completed two speech tasks every fortnight: a scripted task (reading a fixed passage — controls content, isolates acoustic features) and a free response task (describing a picture — allows natural language but is acoustically variable). Results were modelled separately by language and by task — yielding a grid of 6 sets of LMMs.

Participants

Adults with a history of MDD, recruited via the RADAR-MDD study across King's College London (UK), Vrije Universiteit Amsterdam (Netherlands), and FIDMAG (Spain). Inclusion required a confirmed episode of MDD in the past 5 years. The study was naturalistic — participants recorded themselves at home, in their own acoustic environments, using their own smartphones.

The two speech tasks

Scripted task: Read aloud a fixed phonetically balanced passage ("The North Wind and the Sun" — an IPA standard text used globally in linguistics). Advantages: content is identical for all participants, so any acoustic variation reflects the speaker, not what they said. Disadvantages: somewhat artificial, may not reflect natural speech. This task showed stronger LMM associations.

Free response task: Describe a picture for 30–60 seconds. More naturalistic, but content varies — what someone chooses to say also depends on depression, making it harder to isolate acoustic effects from linguistic effects.

The outcome measure

PHQ-8 (Patient Health Questionnaire, 8 items, scored 0–24). Completed fortnightly at the same time as the speech recording. This is the depression severity score used as the predictor in every LMM.

Missing data — and why LMM was essential

Of 585 enrolled participants, only 461 (79%) provided any analysable speech data. Dropout was substantial and correlated with baseline severity in the Spanish cohort. LMMs were essential because they handle unbalanced data: each participant contributed a different number of timepoints (median ~433 days of active recording, IQR 358–473 days). ANOVA would have required complete cases — discarding the partial data from 124 participants entirely.

// Statistical Method — Full Decomposition
The LMM specification, every decision explained

The authors ran one LMM per speech feature (28 features × 3 languages × 2 tasks = up to 168 models). Each model had the same structure:

# The exact model (per feature, per language, per task)
feature_z ~ phq8 + age + height + gender + years_education + (1 | participant_id)

# Fitted with lme4 in R:
lmer(feature_z ~ phq8 + age + height + gender + years_educ + (1|participant), REML=TRUE)

# Coefficients reported as standardised betas with 95% CI
# CIs obtained by bootstrap resampling (1000 iterations) — not parametric
# → robust to non-normality of residuals

Decision 1 — Random intercepts only, not random slopes

Each participant gets their own baseline level for each speech feature (random intercept). The effect of PHQ-8 on voice is assumed the same across all participants (fixed slope). The authors did not fit random slopes for PHQ-8. Why not?

  • Random slopes require more observations per participant. With fortnightly data and substantial dropout, many participants had too few timepoints.
  • The primary scientific question was cohort-level: "Does depression severity correlate with voice features on average?" — not "Does this correlation vary by person?"
  • Random slope models for 168 feature × language × task combinations would have severe convergence issues.
What this means for interpreting the β coefficients

The β on PHQ-8 is a population-average effect: "for a 1 SD increase in this feature across the cohort, PHQ-8 changes by β points, on average, controlling for individual baseline differences." It does not tell you how any individual patient's voice changes with their own depression changes — that would require random slopes or a within-person analysis.

Decision 2 — Feature standardisation before modelling

Each speech feature was standardised (mean = 0, SD = 1) across the whole dataset before entering the LMM. This means:

  • All 28 features are now on the same scale — β coefficients are directly comparable across features.
  • A β of 0.05 for speech rate means the same thing as a β of 0.05 for pause duration: a 1 SD change in the feature associates with a 0.05 unit change in PHQ-8.
  • The intercept term becomes interpretable: it is the expected PHQ-8 when all features are at the cohort average.

Decision 3 — Bootstrap CIs, not parametric

Rather than computing standard errors from the model's Fisher information matrix (the usual approach), the authors used 1000 bootstrap samples to construct confidence intervals. This gives more reliable uncertainty estimates when residuals may not be perfectly normal — as is likely with naturalistically collected speech data.

The covariates — and why they matter

Age, height, gender, and years in education were included as fixed-effect covariates. This is not arbitrary tidying — each has a specific acoustic justification:

Age
Voice changes with age — lower pitch, slower rate, greater breathiness. Older participants would systematically differ in acoustic features regardless of depression.
Height
Taller people tend to have longer vocal tracts — deeper resonant frequencies (lower F1/F2 formants). A confound for spectral features.
Gender
Large pitch differences (F0) between typical male/female voices. Without controlling for gender, pitch features would be dominated by this biological difference, not depression.
Education
Years in education correlates with verbal fluency, articulation rate, and lexical complexity — which affect speech rate and pause features independently of depression severity.

The pause paradox — why the LMM found what others didn't

Many prior studies reported that pause duration and pause rate increase with depression. This paper found no significant association for pause features. The key methodological difference: earlier studies did not account for repeated-measures clustering within participants. Without random intercepts, within-person correlations inflate test statistics — producing false positives. Once the LMM correctly partitioned variance, the pause-depression link evaporated. This is a direct, consequential example of why LMMs matter.

The Spanish cohort — a systematic null result

The Spanish cohort showed no significant associations between any speech feature and PHQ-8 severity — in either task. The paper attributes this to: (1) baseline severity was significantly higher for Spanish participants who provided scripted speech, suggesting selection bias; (2) the passage used ("The North Wind and the Sun") was translated from English and may not have been as phonetically balanced in Spanish; (3) smaller effective sample from the Spanish site. This is an important caution: speech biomarkers may not transfer straightforwardly across languages and cultural contexts even within the same study.

// Critical Appraisal
Strengths and limitations — what the paper itself says, and what it doesn't
✓ Strengths
  • Scale: Largest real-world longitudinal speech dataset in clinical MDD at publication. 585 participants, 18 months, ecological validity — people speaking at home, not in sound booths.
  • Clinical sample: Participants had confirmed MDD history — not students, not MTurk workers, not subclinical populations. Findings are directly applicable to clinical settings.
  • Multilingual: Three languages tested simultaneously in the same study. Allows assessment of which findings generalise beyond English, which is rare in the speech-depression literature.
  • Correct statistics: LMMs with random intercepts — explicitly accounts for repeated-measures clustering. This is why some earlier pause-based results were not replicated: the prior studies used inappropriate methods.
  • Bootstrap CIs: More robust uncertainty estimates than parametric SEs, appropriate given noisy ecological data.
  • Open methods: Feature extraction via Parselmouth (open-source Praat wrapper), models in lme4. Reproducible pipeline.
↯ Limitations
  • Cohort-level effects only: The random intercept model estimates average associations across the cohort. It cannot tell you whether an individual patient's voice is tracking their own depression changes — that requires random slopes or N-of-1 designs. The paper explicitly acknowledges this as a major limitation.
  • No random slopes: Individual variation in the PHQ-8→speech relationship is absorbed into residual error rather than modelled. Some patients may show strong individual-level associations that the cohort-level analysis misses.
  • Recording quality variance: Smartphone recording in varied home environments introduces acoustic noise. No microphone standardisation. This likely adds measurement error that biases β towards zero (attenuation bias).
  • Spanish cohort issues: Differential dropout correlated with severity, potentially different task translation quality. Spanish results should be interpreted with caution.
  • Features averaged over recording: The paper averages acoustic features across the entire recording. Temporal dynamics within a recording (how voice changes from start to end) are lost. These dynamics may be more sensitive to depression than global averages.
  • PHQ-8 as ground truth: PHQ-8 is a self-report scale, not a clinical assessment. It has ceiling and floor effects, and is subject to response bias. Error in the predictor (classical measurement error) attenuates regression coefficients.
// LMM Decisions — Annotated
Every modelling choice mapped to the equation

Using Y = Xβ + Zu + ε as the template, here is where each paper decision lands:

Fixed Effects (Xβ)

X contains: PHQ-8 (the predictor of interest), plus age, height, gender, years_education (covariates). β contains: the population-average effect of each. β_phq8 is the coefficient of interest — it answers "across all participants, does worse depression associate with a different voice?"

Random Effects (Zu)

Z is the participant membership matrix — a 1 in column j for every row belonging to participant j. u is the vector of random intercepts, one per participant: u_Alice, u_Bob, … ~ N(0, σ²_u). These absorb each participant's stable voice baseline. Without u, Alice's naturally slow speech would inflate the speech-rate coefficient.

Residuals (ε)

Within-participant, within-session noise. Captures: recording environment variation, microphone quality, the participant's mood at that exact moment beyond their PHQ-8 score, any other unmodelled source. Bootstrap CIs help because ε is probably not perfectly normal given ecological recording conditions.

The ICC — implied by the model

The paper doesn't report ICC explicitly, but REML estimates σ²_u and σ²_ε for each model. For speech rate, you'd expect ICC ≈ 0.4–0.7 — meaning 40–70% of variance in speech rate is stable across a person's sessions, not fluctuating with depression. This high ICC is precisely why LMMs were non-optional.

// Relevance to Your Work
What this means for your KCL experiment

You are likely picking up where this paper left off

This paper calls out its own main limitation: it estimates population-average (between-person) effects, not individual-level (within-person) change. Its random intercept model cannot tell you whether a specific patient's voice is tracking their specific depression trajectory.

Your work with Dr Cummins at KCL is likely addressing exactly this — either through: (a) random slopes models that estimate each participant's individual PHQ-8→voice slope; (b) N-of-1 study designs analysed with LMMs or Bayesian methods; or (c) more targeted acoustic feature engineering focused on temporal dynamics within recordings rather than global averages.

Use this paper to justify your LMM

Cummins et al. (2023) establishes that LMMs with random intercepts are the correct approach for repeated speech data in depression research. You can cite it directly as methodological precedent — it is from your lab, it uses your data platform, and it uses your exact tools.

Expect to extend the random effects structure

If your study has more observations per participant, you can fit random slopes — allowing each participant's β_phq8 to differ. This addresses the core limitation this paper flags. Whether the data support random slopes depends on your N per person and your ICC estimates.

Pause features are not dead

The null result for pauses at the cohort level doesn't mean pauses are not biomarkers. It means the average effect across people is small or inconsistent. At the individual level, some patients may show strong pause-depression correlations. A random slopes analysis would test this directly.
