The Central Argument

Why LMM, specifically, in Phase 4?

This is the question you need to be able to answer in a seminar or interview. Here's the complete argument:

1. Your data has within-patient correlation — OLS p-values are meaningless+

With 100 patients × 26 timepoints = 2,600 rows, OLS thinks you have 2,600 independent data points. You have 100 independent units. The effective sample size for testing your PHQ-8 coefficient is somewhere between 100 and 2,600, depending on the ICC. OLS gives you the 2,600 version — massively inflated degrees of freedom, far too small standard errors, false positives everywhere.

The ICC for acoustic features in longitudinal voice data is typically 0.3–0.6. At ICC = 0.5, the design effect is approximately 1 + (n_per_group − 1) × ICC = 1 + 25 × 0.5 = 13.5. This means OLS standard errors are off by a factor of √13.5 ≈ 3.7. A t-value of 3.7 in OLS could be a real t-value of 1.0 — totally non-significant.

2. Delta normalisation requires accounting for individual baselines+

You've Z-scored each patient's features relative to their own first few sessions. This delta normalisation converts absolute acoustic values (Alice talks slowly) to within-patient deviation (Alice is talking slower than her personal average). The LMM random intercept is the statistical formalisation of exactly this logic: u_Alice estimates Alice's personal baseline, which the fixed effects then operate on top of.

Using LMM without delta normalisation, or delta normalisation without LMM, are both partial solutions. Together, they ensure that the PHQ-8 coefficient β₁ captures truly within-person change in voice, not between-person baseline differences.

3. Missing data is guaranteed — LMM handles it; ANOVA doesn't+

Patients in a year-long study will miss sessions. You will have unbalanced data. Repeated-measures ANOVA requires complete data (or listwise deletion, which wastes data and introduces bias). LMM via REML uses all available data for each patient and handles missing timepoints gracefully, because it's modelling the underlying trajectory rather than requiring specific timepoints to be present.

4. You get scientifically interpretable estimates, not just predictions+

The ML model in Phase 5 will predict depression state. But it won't tell you why — which acoustic features matter, and how much, in human-understandable units. The LMM Phase 4 provides exactly that: "For every 1-point increase in PHQ-8, pause duration increases by 0.08 SD above personal baseline (95% CI: 0.05–0.11, p < 0.001)." That's a validated biomarker claim. That's publishable science. ML predictions without this statistical foundation are a black box without clinical credibility.

You now understand the full pipeline

Downsampling → VAD → Feature extraction → Delta normalisation → LMM (statistical validation) → ML classification. Every step has a specific statistical rationale. The LMM step is where you prove the science. The ML step is where you build the tool. Without Phase 4, Phase 5 is scientifically indefensible.
