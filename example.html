The Dataset

Meet the data

2
What OLS Gets Wrong

OLS ignores who the observations belong to

First, let's see what happens if we naïvely run OLS regression: pause_z ~ phq8 on all 12 rows, ignoring patient identity entirely.

// Figure 2 — OLS sees all 12 points as independent. One line through everything.
The OLS line (red, dashed): β₀ = −1.02, β₁ = +0.104 per PHQ-8 point. The fit looks reasonable — but the standard errors are wrong, because OLS believes it has 12 independent data points. It has 4 (one per patient). The t-statistic for PHQ-8 is inflated by the false degrees of freedom. Many of the residuals are systematically positive (Alice, Cara) or systematically negative (Bob) — a clear signature of ignored patient structure.

OLS — What It Assumes

All 12 rows are 12 independent people. Alice's week 2 and Alice's week 10 are as unrelated as Alice and a stranger. The residuals are random noise with no structure. Standard errors computed from n=12 independent observations.

LMM — What We Know

We have 4 independent patients, each seen 3 times. Within-patient rows are correlated. Alice's rows share her baseline voice. LMM adds a patient-specific intercept (random effect) that absorbs this correlation. Standard errors computed correctly.

3
Building the LMM

Specifying the model

We fit: pause_z ~ phq8 + (1 | patient)

This means: PHQ-8 as a fixed effect (same slope for everyone), and a random intercept per patient (each person's baseline is allowed to differ). Written out in full for Row 1 (Alice, week 2):

# The LMM equation for Row i, patient j:
Yij = β₀ + β₁×PHQ8ij + uj + εij

# Row 1: Alice (j=Alice), week 2
0.60 = β₀ + β₁×12 + u_Alice + ε₁

# β₀, β₁ are SHARED across all patients (fixed effects)
# u_Alice is UNIQUE to Alice (random intercept)
# ε₁ is the remaining residual noise

The X matrix (fixed effects design)

One column of 1s (intercept), one column of PHQ-8 values. Every patient, every row:

// Figure 3 — The X matrix: 12 rows × 2 columns
X encodes the predictors. β₀ (intercept column) applies to every row. β₁ (PHQ-8 column) scales by each row's PHQ-8 value. The same β vector multiplies every row — that's what "fixed" means.

The Z matrix (random effects design)

Z encodes which patient each row belongs to. Four patients → four columns. A 1 in column j means "this row belongs to patient j — apply their u offset here."

// Figure 4 — The Z matrix: 12 rows × 4 patients
Each row has exactly one 1 — in the column corresponding to that row's patient. Everything else is 0. When you multiply Z × u, each row of Y gets its patient's random intercept pasted in. The Z matrix is purely a routing/membership table.
4
Estimation (REML)

What the model estimates

REML finds the values of β₀, β₁, σ²_u, and σ²_ε that make the observed data most probable. I'll give you the results here — in real software you'd run lmer(pause_z ~ phq8 + (1|patient), REML=TRUE).

Fixed Effects
(Intercept) β₀ −0.862 SE = 0.241 t = −3.58
phq8        β₁ +0.107 SE = 0.019 t = +5.63 ★
Random Effects
σ²_u  (between-patient variance) 0.342 SD = 0.585
σ²_ε  (residual within-patient) 0.014 SD = 0.118
ICC = σ²_u / (σ²_u + σ²_ε) 0.342 / 0.356 = 0.96 Very high — patient identity dominates

ICC = 0.96 means 96% of the variance in pause length is due to which patient is being measured, not within-patient change. Random intercepts are absolutely essential here — without them, OLS standard errors would be off by a factor of √(1 + (3−1)×0.96) ≈ √2.92 ≈ 1.7.

The random intercept estimates (BLUPs)

The model also estimates each patient's personal offset — called BLUPs (Best Linear Unbiased Predictors). These tell you how much each patient's baseline deviates from the population average.

u_Alice = +0.579 ← Alice pauses 0.58 SD more than average patient at same PHQ-8
u_Bob = −0.436 ← Bob pauses 0.44 SD less than average
u_Cara = +0.892 ← Cara pauses 0.89 SD more than average
u_Dan = −0.035 ← Dan is close to average baseline

# Note: BLUPs are shrunk toward zero compared to raw per-patient means.
# The model "borrows strength" — extreme patients are pulled slightly toward
# the population mean. This is called partial pooling.

Notice the residual pattern

Alice's and Cara's residuals are all negative — the model consistently over-predicts them. Bob's are consistently positive. This is a signature of the BLUPs not being perfectly estimated — with only n=3 per patient, there's uncertainty. With more data per patient, the BLUPs would be more accurate and residuals more centred.

7
Residual Diagnostics

Checking the model assumptions

A model is only trustworthy if its assumptions hold. Three key plots:

// Figure 6 — Residuals vs. fitted values (should show no pattern)
What to look for: Points randomly scattered around zero (horizontal dashed line), with no trumpet shape (heteroscedasticity) and no curve (non-linearity). If residuals fan out at higher fitted values, your variance is not constant. Here we see mild clustering by patient — a reminder that n=4 patients is tiny; with 100 patients this would look cleaner.
// Figure 7 — Random effects (BLUPs) vs. population mean — the patient-level variance
The BLUPs plotted on a number line. The spread here estimates σ_u = 0.585. These should be approximately normally distributed around zero — the model's assumption about random effects. With 4 patients we can't really check normality, but the pattern looks plausible.
8
Interpretation

What do these numbers actually mean?

β₁ = +0.107 — The Core Finding

For every 1-point increase in PHQ-8, pause length increases by 0.107 SD above that patient's personal baseline. A patient going from PHQ-8 = 8 (mild) to PHQ-8 = 18 (severe) is expected to increase their pause length by 10 × 0.107 = 1.07 SD above their personal average. That is a very large effect — clinically meaningful, statistically strong (t = 5.63).

u_Alice = +0.579 — What the BLUP Tells You

At any given PHQ-8 score, Alice tends to pause 0.579 SD longer than the average patient. This is not depression — it's just Alice's natural speaking style. The model has learned to subtract this out, so the β₁ estimate purely captures change with depression, not "Alice is a slow talker." Without the random intercept, Alice's elevated baseline would partially inflate the PHQ-8 coefficient.

ICC = 0.96 — A Sobering Number

96% of variance in pause_z is attributable to stable patient identity. Only 4% is within-patient fluctuation. This means: knowing who the patient is predicts their pause length far better than knowing their current PHQ-8 score. The biomarker exists (β₁ is real), but the signal-to-noise ratio for within-patient change is low. In a real study this would motivate either more timepoints per patient, or looking for features with lower ICC (more within-patient variability).
